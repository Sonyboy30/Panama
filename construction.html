Construction & Challenges

Innovation, disease control, and determination defined the canal’s construction era.

When the United States began construction of the Panama Canal in 1904, Chief Engineer John Stevens and later George Washington Goethals led the effort to overcome immense natural and logistical obstacles.

The American engineers abandoned the French plan for a sea-level canal, opting instead for a lock-and-dam system to manage the drastic changes in elevation and control flooding. This decision, along with new excavation machinery and careful organization, made the project feasible.

Early on, diseases such as malaria and yellow fever devastated the workforce. Dr. William Gorgas implemented a revolutionary sanitation campaign—draining swamps, fumigating buildings, and eliminating mosquito breeding grounds—that virtually eradicated these illnesses and made sustained work possible.

By 1913, workers had excavated over 200 million cubic yards of earth. Despite landslides, intense heat, and dangerous explosions, the canal opened the following year, symbolizing one of the greatest engineering feats in history.
