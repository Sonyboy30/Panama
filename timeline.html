Timeline of the Panama Canal

Key moments from the dream to the completion of the Canal (1869–1914).

1869 — The Vision Begins

French engineer Ferdinand de Lesseps completes the Suez Canal in Egypt. The same year, U.S. President Ulysses S. Grant commissions a study of a canal route through Central America.

1879–1888 — The French Attempt

De Lesseps begins construction of a sea-level canal in Panama. His company is devastated by disease, landslides, and bankruptcy. The French effort collapses after over 20,000 deaths and financial ruin.

1903 — Panama Gains Independence

The U.S. supports Panama’s independence from Colombia and signs the Hay–Bunau-Varilla Treaty, granting rights to build and control the Canal Zone.

1904–1905 — The American Effort Begins

Chief Engineer John Wallace starts work but resigns amid poor conditions. Dr. William Gorgas leads sanitation campaigns, eradicating yellow fever and malaria through mosquito control.

1905–1907 — Stevens and Sanitation

John Stevens reorganizes the project, prioritizing infrastructure, housing, and safety. Roosevelt’s 1906 visit becomes a symbol of determination. The decision to build a lock-based canal sets the path to success.

1907–1913 — Goethals Leads the Charge

Under George Washington Goethals, excavation at Culebra Cut and construction of the Gatún Dam proceed rapidly. Thousands of workers labor around the clock, enduring danger and heat.

August 15, 1914 — The Canal Opens

The SS Ancon becomes the first ship to pass through the Panama Canal. Though World War I overshadows the event, the canal stands as one of the greatest engineering achievements in history.
